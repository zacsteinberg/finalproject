Movie Data Analysis

Overall Introduction

Movies are a form of entertainment that has existed since the end of the 19th century. They have evolved drastically, including sound, color, animation, and computer-generated images (CGI). At the same time, there have been different trends, including different genres being popular and different companies producing more movies. However, with all these changes, movies have remained a central part of culture worldwide. Just in the past year, the success of movies like Barbie and Oppenheimer were massive stories. They have won many awards, generated billions in revenue, and led to increased attention on each subject matter. Therefore, we want to analyze data from thousands of movies over the past century by performing time, location, genre, and popularity-based analyses. By investigating these topics and visualizing the results, we will better understand how movies differ and what drives popularity and success among different movies.

Different metrics that we will use to analyze the movie data include popularity, number of votes, budget, release year, runtime, genre, and profit. We can measure success in movies by looking at those that have high profit and have a high popularity score with a significantly high number of votes.

To learn more about movies and different metrics of success, you can visit the IMDB website and read this study from the Carnegie Mellon University.

Introduction to the data

To answer our questions and complete our analysis, we found detailed data for a large universe of movies. The Movie Database is a powerful database that is useful for this task. We preprocessed the data to keep approximately twelve attributes for 6000 movies from The Movie Database released from 1916 to 2023, with a focus on movies produced in the 21st century. The primary attributes we use are budget, revenue, genre(s), popularity, number of votes/ratings, production country, release date/year, runtime, production country, and production companies. After loading the data, we saved the data in a Pandas dataframe with identifiable columns that we used for Altair, Matplotlib, and D3 visualizations. Through our data preprocessing, we also derived some new data points such as profit, main genre, and decade released.

To improve our data visualizations, we performed preprocessing on the data. We dropped columns that were not relevant for our analysis, dropped any rows with blank data, cleaned up the formatting, and created new columns for our visualizations.

The first dimension we wanted to examine our data on was production country, so we created an interactive horizontal bar chart of the number of movies produced in each country. It allows you to select and highlight the bars of individual or multiple countries in red.

In general, the vast majority of the movies in the dataset were produced in the United States. This is due to the presence of Hollywood and how the entertainment industry is centered in the United States. It is the biggest market for movies in the world as well, and if a movie is successful in the US, it is likely going to be successful worldwide. Other countries that were among the top producers include the UK, Canada, and Germany.

Profit Analysis

The above chart shows the distribution of American movies by their profit. We decided to only look at American movies because those were the most common by far of any primary production country. It is clear from this chart the vast majority of movies lose money and it is rare for movies to make a lot of money.

The relationship between budget, popularity, and genre

Next, we wanted to begin to perform our genre-based analysis. We first created an interactive bubble plot that shows the popularity of movies against the budget. The colors of the bubble represents the genre and there is also a radio so the movie genres can be analyzed individually. The size of the bubble represents the number of votes the movie received. When the user hovers over each bubble, it says the name of the movie and the corresponding revenue. Of note, we condensed the number of genres by grouping similar ones together. For instance, the Action genre includes movies primarily classified as "action", "adventure", "science fiction", and "fantasy".

The chart shows that there is a decently strong correlation between budget and popularity. Additionally, vote count seems to have no impact on popularity and higher budget movies do not necessarily get higher vote counts. Lastly, we can see that most movies have a budget between $100-$200 million.

We have also created a stacked bar plot that shows the count of movies by genre produced in the 21st century. It shows how there is been an increase in the proportion of action and horror movies while a decrease in drama.

Comparing Movie Length Across Genres

Lastly, we created an interactive chart that shows the average runtime by genre for movies in a single year. The user can select any year between 2000 and 2023 using the dropdown to explore which genres averaged longer in different years, and to see trends in average runtime for different genres over time. For instance, documentaries consistently have the shortest average runtime almost every year.

Summary and additional work

In this visualization analysis, we explore what leads to the success of a movie and how they have differed over time and across genres. Our analysis shows how the factors of runtime, profit, and popularity, among others differ across genres.

One limitation of our analysis was we only used the first genre in the list of genre classifications for each movie to avoid overlap or repetition. However, this means movies that truly belong in at least two categories (e.g., romantic comedies, aka romcoms) we could not categorize completely accurately. Furthermore, our method of main genre selection was on the assumption that the first genre in the list was the primary genre, which is most likely true, but we are unable to verify that with complete certainty.
